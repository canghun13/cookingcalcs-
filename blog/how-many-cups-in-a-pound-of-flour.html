How Many Cups in a Pound of Flour?

Updated May 2026 · 5 min read

One of the most common questions when baking from a US recipe is how to convert pounds of flour into cups — or the reverse. The answer isn't a single number. It depends entirely on the type of flour, and even on how you fill the cup. Here's the complete breakdown.

The Short Answer

One pound of all-purpose flour equals approximately 3⅓ cups when measured using the spoon-and-level method. But that number changes significantly for other flour types — which is exactly why weighing flour is almost always more accurate than measuring by volume.

Cups per Pound by Flour Type

Flour Type Grams per Cup Cups per Pound
All-purpose flour120g3⅓ cups
Bread flour120g3⅓ cups
Cake flour100g4 cups
Whole wheat flour130g3½ cups
Rye flour102g~4 cups
Almond flour96g~4¼ cups

Why the Number Varies So Much

Flour isn't a uniform substance — each type has a different density, particle size, and tendency to compact. Cake flour is finely milled and light, so a cup of it weighs less than a cup of denser whole wheat flour. This is the core reason why measuring flour by volume is unreliable for anything that requires precision.

On top of that, how you fill the cup matters enormously. Scooping directly from the bag compresses the flour and can add 20–30% more weight than the spoon-and-level method. The figures in the table above use the spoon-and-level standard (spoon flour into the cup, then level off the top with a straight edge).

How to Measure Flour Accurately

If you need to convert a recipe that calls for pounds and you only have measuring cups, use the figures in the table above as a starting point — but be precise about your filling method. For anything involving more than 2–3 cups of flour, weigh it if at all possible.

If you don't have a kitchen scale, the spoon-and-level method is the most consistent cup-based approach:

  1. Fluff the flour in its bag or container with a spoon first — it settles during storage.
  2. Spoon the flour into your measuring cup without packing or tapping.
  3. Level off the top with the back of a straight knife or bench scraper.
  4. Don't tap the cup or shake it to settle the flour — this compresses it.

Common Recipe Amounts at a Glance

For all-purpose flour specifically, here are the most useful reference points:

  • 1 pound flour = 3⅓ cups = 454g
  • 5 pounds flour (standard bag) = about 16⅔ cups
  • 2 cups flour = approximately 0.6 pounds = 240g
  • 3 cups flour = approximately 0.9 pounds = 360g

When Precision Really Matters

For most bread recipes and all pastry, working in grams is significantly more reliable. A 10% variation in flour weight — which is easy to hit with volume measuring — is enough to change the texture of bread noticeably and can collapse a pastry dough.

For simple recipes like muffins, pancakes, or coarser breads, cup measurements work fine. Save the kitchen scale for anything where texture and rise really matter: croissants, sourdough, macarons, chiffon cake.

Scooping vs. Spooning: Why the Same "1 Cup" Isn't the Same Amount

Dipping the measuring cup straight into the bag compacts the flour and typically packs in 15-20% more than the standard spoon-and-level method this guide's numbers are based on. That difference alone explains most "why is my cake so dense" and "why did my bread come out dry" problems — the recipe wasn't wrong, the cup of flour it called for was 20-30g heavier than intended. Spoon the flour lightly into the cup until it mounds above the rim, then level it off with a straight edge, and don't tap or shake the cup first.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many cups in a 2-pound bag of flour?

A 2-pound bag of all-purpose flour contains approximately 6⅔ cups. A 5-pound bag contains about 16⅔ cups.

How many cups in a pound of self-rising flour?

Self-rising flour has approximately the same density as all-purpose flour — about 3⅓ cups per pound. The added baking powder and salt don't significantly change the volume.

Is a cup of flour always the same weight?

No. Depending on how the cup is filled, a cup of all-purpose flour can weigh anywhere from 120g (spoon-and-level) to 160g (scooped directly). This is why two bakers following the same recipe can get different results if they measure differently.

Storing Flour to Keep Density Consistent

Flour settles and compacts over time in storage, which is part of why scooping directly from a bag that's been sitting for weeks yields more flour per cup than a freshly opened bag. Stirring or fluffing flour before measuring — even when using the spoon-and-level method — helps keep results consistent regardless of how long the bag has been open. For the most reliable results across different bags and storage times, weighing on a scale removes this variable entirely.

Does sifted flour weigh less per cup than unsifted?

Yes, noticeably. Sifting aerates flour and reduces its density, so a cup of sifted flour can weigh 15-20% less than a cup of the same flour unsifted. If a recipe specifies "1 cup sifted flour," sift first and then measure — sifting after measuring gives a different (larger) actual amount than the recipe intends.

Does humidity affect how much flour a cup holds?

Slightly. Flour absorbs a small amount of ambient moisture in humid conditions, which can make it clump and pack marginally differently than in dry conditions. The effect is generally small enough to ignore for home baking, but it's part of why professional bakeries in humid climates sometimes adjust flour weights seasonally.
