How Many Grams in a Cup of Rice?

Updated May 2026 · 5 min read

Rice is one of the most common ingredients where cup-to-gram conversions actually matter. Whether you're scaling a recipe, tracking nutrition, or following a recipe from a different country, knowing the exact gram weight of a cup of rice saves you from guesswork. The answer depends on the type of rice and whether it's dry or cooked.

Grams per Cup — Cooked Rice

Cooked rice is significantly heavier than dry rice because it absorbs water during cooking. A cup of cooked white rice weighs approximately 175–195g, and cooked brown rice weighs approximately 195–210g.

This matters when following nutritional information or recipes that specify cooked rice. A recipe that calls for "1 cup cooked rice" is not the same as "1 cup dry rice" — dry rice roughly triples in volume and roughly doubles in weight when cooked.

Why Rice Type Changes the Weight

Different rice varieties have different grain sizes, densities, and shapes. Short-grain rice (like sushi rice) packs more tightly into a cup than long-grain varieties like basmati, which have more air space between grains. Wild rice is notably lighter because the grains are long and thin with a hollow center.

For everyday cooking, these differences are small enough that using the standard 185–200g range for dry white rice is fine. For precise nutritional tracking or recipe development, use the specific figure for the rice type you're working with.

How Much Does 1 Cup of Dry Rice Yield Cooked?

This is one of the most practical rice conversion questions. As a general rule:

  • 1 cup dry white rice → approximately 3 cups cooked (around 555g)
  • 1 cup dry brown rice → approximately 2.5–3 cups cooked
  • 1 cup dry basmati → approximately 3 cups cooked (basmati expands more than most)
  • 1 cup dry wild rice → approximately 3.5 cups cooked

These ratios vary slightly depending on how much water you use and how long you cook it. Absorption methods typically yield a bit less than boil-and-drain methods.

How to Measure Rice Accurately

For dry rice, use a standard dry measuring cup and level it off. Don't pack or shake the cup — rice settles slightly during measurement, which can add 5–10% extra. For anything requiring precision (nutritional tracking, scaling large batches), weigh it on a kitchen scale.

For cooked rice, pack it lightly into the measuring cup. Cooked rice compresses easily, so a packed cup can weigh noticeably more than a loosely filled one.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many grams is ½ cup of dry white rice?

½ cup of dry long-grain white rice is approximately 92–93g. Half of the standard 185g per cup.

How many grams is 1 cup of jasmine rice?

1 cup of dry jasmine rice is approximately 185g — the same as long-grain white rice, since jasmine is a long-grain variety.

How many cups is 100g of rice?

100g of dry white rice is approximately 0.54 cups, or just over half a cup.

Does the measurement change between white and brown rice?

Slightly. Brown rice has the outer bran layer intact, making the grains slightly bulkier. 1 cup of dry brown rice weighs about 190g versus 185g for white — a small but real difference that matters when scaling large batches.

Rice-to-Water Ratio by Type

Since cooked weight depends heavily on how much water the rice absorbs, the water ratio you use affects the final gram weight per cup. Standard ratios: white rice uses about 1¾ cups water per cup of dry rice, brown rice uses closer to 2¼ cups (needing more time and water due to the bran layer), and basmati uses about 1½ cups since it's a lower-starch variety that doesn't need to absorb as much liquid to cook through.

Why does basmati rice yield more cooked rice per cup than other varieties?

Basmati grains are unusually long and slender, and during cooking they elongate significantly (up to twice their raw length) rather than just swelling in width like shorter grain varieties. This elongation is why basmati produces noticeably more cooked volume per cup of dry rice than short or medium-grain varieties, even though the raw dry weight per cup is similar.

Does rinsing rice before cooking change the gram weight?

Rinsing removes surface starch but adds a small amount of surface moisture, which roughly cancels out for dry-weight purposes — rinse before measuring rather than after for the most consistent results, since rinsed-then-measured rice can weigh slightly more due to residual water clinging to the grains.
