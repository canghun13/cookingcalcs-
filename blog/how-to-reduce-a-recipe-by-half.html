How to Cut a Recipe in Half — Every Ingredient Covered

June 2026 · 6 min read

Halving a recipe sounds simple — divide everything by 2. And for most ingredients, it is. But eggs don't split neatly, leavening behaves differently in small quantities, and a can of tomatoes comes in one size regardless of what your math says. Here's how to halve a recipe properly, ingredient by ingredient.

How to Halve Eggs

This is where most people get stuck. You can't use half a shell egg directly, but you can measure the volume:

  • 1 large egg = about 3 tablespoons when lightly beaten
  • Half a large egg = 1½ tablespoons
  • 1 egg yolk = about 1¼ tablespoons
  • 1 egg white = about 2 tablespoons

To get half an egg: crack it into a small bowl, beat briefly with a fork until combined, then measure out 1½ tablespoons. Use the rest for scrambled eggs or another recipe the same day.

For most cookies and quick breads, you can round: use 1 whole egg instead of ½ and the result will be slightly more moist but still good. For more precise baking — custards, tarts, soufflés — measure by volume.

Halving Baking Powder and Baking Soda

These scale linearly — halve them exactly. Do not round up "just to be safe." Too much baking soda creates a metallic, soapy taste. Too much baking powder makes baked goods rise quickly then collapse.

If your halved amount is something awkward like ⅜ tsp, use ¼ tsp and add a small pinch more. A ⅛ tsp measuring spoon is worth buying if you bake regularly — it removes the guesswork entirely.

Halving Spices and Salt

For spices, start at 60–70% of the calculated half, then taste. Strong spices — cayenne, chili flakes, cinnamon, cloves — are easy to overdo in small batches. The math gives you the right proportion, but your palate is the final check.

Salt is the exception: halve it precisely and adjust at the end after tasting. Under-salted is easy to fix; over-salted isn't.

What to Do with Canned Goods

Cans come in fixed sizes. If a recipe calls for one 14oz can of tomatoes and you're halving it, you have three options:

  1. Use the whole can and increase the number of servings accordingly
  2. Use half the can and refrigerate the rest (use within 3–4 days, transferred to a container)
  3. Scale the rest of the recipe up to use the full can

Option 1 is usually the easiest for soups, sauces, and stews. Option 2 works if you'll use the rest soon.

Pan Size When Halving a Baking Recipe

A halved recipe needs a pan with about half the surface area of the original. This maintains the same batter depth and ensures similar baking time and texture.

If you don't have the right pan size, you can use the original pan — but fill it less and check for doneness 10–15 minutes early. The thinner layer will cook faster.

Cooking Time When Halving

For baked goods in the correct pan size: time stays roughly the same. The batter depth is what drives baking time, not total volume.

For stovetop dishes (soups, sauces, stews): expect 15–25% less time. A smaller volume heats faster and reaches temperature sooner. Watch more closely than usual.

For roasted or braised meats: if you've cut the protein weight in half, reduce time proportionally, but use internal temperature as your final check rather than time. Cooking time is a guideline; temperature is the truth.

When Halving Doesn't Work Well

Some recipes resist halving:

  • Yeast bread: Small dough quantities don't proof the same way. Better to make the full batch and freeze half the baked loaves.
  • Candy and caramel: Small quantities of sugar syrup heat unevenly and are harder to control.
  • Deep-fry recipes: You need enough oil to maintain temperature — halving the oil can result in temperature drops that make food greasy.

For these, make the full recipe. Most finished goods freeze well — bread, muffins, cookies, soups, and casseroles all hold up for 2–3 months in the freezer.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I halve ¾ cup?

Half of ¾ cup is 6 tablespoons. Since ¾ cup = 12 tablespoons, half is 6 tablespoons. You can also measure it as ¼ cup + 2 tablespoons, which is the same thing.

Can I just halve the cooking time when I halve a recipe?

Not automatically. For baked goods in the right pan, time stays similar. For stovetop cooking, you'll finish faster. Always check doneness — use visual cues, a toothpick test, or an internal temperature reading rather than relying on a halved time estimate.

What's the hardest ingredient to halve?

Eggs, because they're discrete units. A close second is leavening — baking powder and baking soda need to be halved precisely, and the resulting amounts are small enough that measurement error matters more than usual.

Should I reduce oven temperature when halving a baking recipe?

Only if you're using a smaller pan. If you reduce the pan size correctly (roughly half the surface area), keep the oven temperature the same. If you're using the original pan with a thinner layer of batter, reduce temperature by 25°F and check earlier.
