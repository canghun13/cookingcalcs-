How to Reduce a Recipe to Fewer Servings

Updated May 2026 · 5 min read

Most recipes are written for 4–6 servings. If you're cooking for one or two, you're either eating the same meal for days or throwing food away. Here's how to cut a recipe down properly — without ruining proportions or wasting ingredients.

The Basic Formula

Divide the number of servings you want by the number in the recipe. If a recipe makes 6 and you want 2, your factor is 2÷6 = 0.33. Multiply every ingredient by 0.33. Our Recipe Multiplier does this automatically for every ingredient at once.

How to Cut a Recipe in Half

Halving is the most common reduction. For most ingredients, simply divide by 2. The trickier parts:

  • 1 egg → ½ egg: Beat one egg lightly in a small bowl. Use 2 tablespoons (half the volume of a large egg).
  • Odd tablespoon amounts: 3 tbsp becomes 1½ tbsp. 1½ tbsp becomes 2¼ tsp (since 1 tbsp = 3 tsp).
  • Canned goods: You can't use half a can easily. Either scale to use the full can and freeze the extra, or store the remainder for another recipe within a few days.

Handling Eggs

Eggs are the hardest ingredient to reduce because you can't easily use a fraction of one. For a half-recipe that calls for 1 egg, beat the egg lightly and use half. For a third-recipe that calls for 2 eggs, use 1 egg and a splash of milk to compensate for the missing moisture.

If a reduced recipe calls for something like 1.5 eggs, round to 1 egg for drier baked goods (cookies, scones) or use 2 for moister ones (cakes, quick breads) where an extra egg won't hurt.

Leavening Agents

Baking powder and baking soda scale directly — use exactly the proportional amount. These don't have the same issue as spices where halving can still be too much. Use a measuring spoon and be precise. Even a small excess of baking soda creates a soapy, bitter taste.

Spices and Salt

Start with 75% of the calculated amount for spices and salt, then taste and adjust. This is especially important with strong spices like cayenne, cinnamon, and cloves, which can dominate a dish even in small quantities. You can always add more, but you can't take it out.

Pan Size Matters

When halving a baking recipe, you'll need a smaller pan. Use a pan with roughly half the surface area. An 8×8 pan works for half a recipe that calls for a 9×13. For cakes, use a 6-inch round pan instead of an 8-inch.

With a smaller pan, reduce your oven temperature by 25°F and start checking for doneness 10 minutes early, since the smaller volume cooks faster.

Cooking Time Adjustments

For stovetop dishes (soups, sauces, braises), half the recipe will cook faster. Watch closely and reduce the time estimate by 15–25%. For baked goods in a properly sized pan, the time stays roughly the same — the batter depth is what determines baking time, not the volume.

When It's Easier Not to Reduce

Some recipes don't reduce well. Bread dough behaves differently at small quantities. Some sauces require a minimum volume to cook properly. In these cases, it's often easier to make the full recipe and freeze half. Most soups, stews, casseroles, muffins, and quick breads freeze very well for 2–3 months.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I reduce a recipe by a third?

Multiply every ingredient by 0.33. For a recipe that serves 6, a third gives you 2 servings. The same rules apply — be precise with leavening, start conservative with spices, and handle eggs by measurement rather than count.

Can I reduce a recipe to 1 serving?

Yes, but some recipes are difficult to scale that small. Baked goods are the hardest — small quantities of batter or dough behave differently, and single-serving amounts can be hard to measure accurately. For cooking (not baking), single-serving reductions usually work well.
