Lamb Chops vs Pork Chops: Cooking Time & Temperature Differences

July 2026 · 7 min read

Lamb chops and pork chops look similar on the cutting board — both are small, bone-in cuts that cook fast — but they don't cook the same way. The target temperature is different, the accepted doneness range is different, and as a result, the actual cook time at the same thickness is meaningfully different too. Here's the side-by-side breakdown.

Target Temperature: The Core Difference

Lamb ChopsPork Chops
USDA minimum safe temp145°F (63°C)145°F (63°C)
Typical target135-145°F (medium-rare to medium)145°F (single target)
Doneness range accepted?Yes — rare to well-done, like beefNo — cooked to one target, not a range

Both cuts share the same USDA minimum of 145°F, but lamb is treated more like beef or steak in practice: many cooks pull rib and loin chops at 135-140°F for a pink, tender center, well below the official minimum, the same way a steak is often served medium-rare. Pork doesn't have that same cultural tradition in the US — 145°F is treated as the target to hit, not a floor to stay above. This single difference in target temperature is the main reason lamb chops finish faster than pork chops of the same thickness.

Across every method, lamb chops finish roughly 20-25% faster than pork chops at the same thickness — a direct result of the roughly 10°F lower target temperature most cooks use for lamb. For exact times by thickness and doneness level, see the full lamb chops cooking time guide and pork chops cooking time guide.

Oven times aren't directly comparable the same way, since oven cook time depends heavily on whether the chop was seared first (much faster finish) or baked straight from raw (longer, since there's no head start from the sear) — both approaches work for either cut, but the numbers you'll see quoted assume different starting points.

Why the Cook Time Actually Differs

Target temperature is the main driver. Going from 135°F (lamb medium-rare) to 145°F (pork) doesn't sound like a big jump, but at the size and thickness of a typical chop, that extra 10°F takes a meaningfully longer time to reach — heat has to fully penetrate a small piece of meat, and the last 10 degrees near the target temperature happen more slowly than the first 100.

Fat content plays a smaller role. Lamb chops (especially rib and loin cuts) tend to have less internal marbling than pork chops, which cook slightly faster with less total mass of fat to render.

Can You Substitute One for the Other?

You can swap lamb chops and pork chops in most recipes that call for pan-searing, grilling, or air frying — the technique is nearly identical — but don't reuse the exact time from the original recipe without adjusting. A recipe written for lamb chops will undercook if you swap in pork chops without adding roughly 20-25% more time, since pork needs to reach the higher 145°F target. Going the other direction — using lamb in a pork recipe — risks overcooking the lamb past medium if you follow the pork chop's longer time exactly. Use a thermometer either way rather than relying on the original recipe's clock time.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do lamb chops cook faster than pork chops?

Yes, at the same thickness and method, lamb chops generally finish about 20-25% faster than pork chops. This is mostly because lamb chops are typically cooked to a lower target — 135-140°F for medium-rare — while pork chops are cooked to a single USDA target of 145°F, roughly 10°F higher.

Can you cook lamb chops medium-rare like a steak, but not pork chops?

Yes. Lamb, like beef, has a range of accepted doneness levels from rare to well-done, and many cooks prefer lamb chops at 135-140°F for a pink, tender center. Pork in the US doesn't have the same rare/medium-rare tradition — the USDA safe minimum of 145°F is treated as the single target, not a range, since older cultural caution around trichinosis (now largely a non-issue with modern pork) still shapes how most home cooks approach it.

Can I substitute pork chops for lamb chops in a recipe, or vice versa?

You can, but adjust the time and expect a different result. Because pork cooks to a higher target temperature, a recipe written for lamb chops will need roughly 20-25% more time if you swap in pork chops of the same thickness, and vice versa. The flavor will also be noticeably different — lamb has a distinct, fattier flavor profile that pork doesn't replicate.

Which is safer to undercook, lamb chops or pork chops?

Lamb has more tolerance for lower internal temperatures — it's safe and commonly served at 135-140°F. Pork should be cooked to the USDA minimum of 145°F followed by a rest; modern pork production has made trichinosis extremely rare, but 145°F remains the recommended safe target regardless.
