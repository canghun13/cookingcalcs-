Salmon vs Shrimp: Cooking Time Differences

July 2026 · 6 min read

Salmon and shrimp both cook to the exact same safe temperature, but they don't take anywhere close to the same amount of time. If you've ever tried to time a dish with both, you already know shrimp is done and salmon is nowhere close. Here's why, and exactly how the numbers compare.

Same Target Temperature, Very Different Speed

SalmonShrimp
FDA safe internal temp145°F (63°C)145°F (63°C)
Typical thickness/size1 inch fillet (standard)Large shrimp, ~½ inch at the widest point
Doneness cueOpaque, flakes easilyPink, opaque, curled into a C shape

Both proteins share the same target — there's no temperature difference driving the gap in cook time, unlike some protein comparisons. The gap is entirely about size: a standard salmon fillet is roughly twice as thick as a large shrimp, and heat takes disproportionately longer to reach the center of a thicker piece of food.

MethodSalmon (1-inch fillet)Shrimp (large, 26/30 count)

Oven/baked (400°F)12–15 min6–8 min
Grill7–9 min total4–6 min total

Across every method, salmon takes roughly twice as long as shrimp — a direct result of that thickness difference, not anything about the fish versus shellfish itself. For exact times by exact thickness or shrimp size, see the full salmon cooking time guide and shrimp cooking time guide.

Cooking Them Together

A common mistake in shrimp-and-salmon dishes — paella, sheet-pan bakes, seafood boils — is adding both at the same time and ending up with rubbery shrimp waiting around for salmon to finish. Stagger the additions instead: start the salmon first, and add the shrimp once the salmon is roughly halfway to two-thirds done. For a sheet-pan bake at 400°F with a 1-inch salmon fillet, that means adding large shrimp around the 6-8 minute mark so both finish within a minute or two of each other.

Why the Margin for Error Is Different

Shrimp's short total cook time means there's very little room between "just done" and "overcooked" — often less than a minute, since the whole cook only takes 2-8 minutes depending on method and size. Salmon has more forgiveness, typically 2-3 minutes of margin, simply because the total cook time is longer to begin with, so a small timing error is a smaller fraction of the total. This is why shrimp benefits more from active attention — pull it the moment it turns pink and curls — while salmon gives you slightly more room to multitask.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does shrimp cook so much faster than salmon?

Both cook to the same FDA-recommended 145°F, so temperature isn't the difference — size is. A large shrimp is only about ⅓ to ½ inch thick at its widest point, while a standard salmon fillet is a full inch. Heat needs to penetrate much less material to reach the center of a shrimp, so it cooks in minutes instead of the 10+ minutes a salmon fillet needs.

Can I cook salmon and shrimp together in the same dish?

Yes, but stagger when you add them — start the salmon first since it takes longer, and add the shrimp partway through so both finish at the same time. In a sheet-pan bake, add shrimp about 6-8 minutes after the salmon goes in, depending on shrimp size.

Do salmon and shrimp cook to the same temperature?

Yes — the FDA recommends 145°F (63°C) for both. The difference between them is entirely about how long it takes to reach that temperature, driven by size and thickness, not a different target.

Which is easier to overcook, salmon or shrimp?

Shrimp, in practical terms — its short cook time means the window between perfectly done and overcooked (rubbery) is often less than a minute. Salmon has more margin, typically 2-3 minutes between done and dry, simply because its total cook time is longer to begin with.
